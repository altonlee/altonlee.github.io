On my 6th birthday, I received a Game Boy Advance with a copy of Super Mario Brothers 3. It was the first electronic toy I had ever received. Opening up the top and powering it on was a portal to a whole new world of fire flowers and bouncing turtles. I just marveled at the idea of pressing a button and making a character on the screen jump at my command. It was from this experience that I declared my love for video games. 

From then on, I got all sorts of games and systems to play on, and I was the happiest little kid in all of Kaimuki. But then fifth grade came around and it was middle school preparation day, and a question had come up in my life that I never thought I would have to answer for the next 8 years of my life:

“What do you want to be when you grow up?”

Honestly, I have never had a great ambition for much else; I never enjoyed drawing, or doing math, outdoor sports, or reading a book. I figured I could just blow it off and continue my lax life of hanging out with friends and playing video games all the time. But my addiction to video games had only gotten worse; I started playing games on the family computer almost daily, my grades were dropping, and my parents weren’t very thrilled about any of this. But it was from my transition to computer games that really piqued my interest in computers in general. I learned to (lightly) code when I wanted to learn how to create my own video game in Unity, doing texture work to replace character textures in a zombie game, and changing the weights and powers of items in another Mario game. 

<img class="ui medium right floated image" src="../images/essay1-mods.jpg">

The final push to computer science wasn’t a push for computer science at all, actually. In my final year of high school, all of my friends, family, and counselors had convinced me that staying at home and pursuing the engineering program at UH would be successful and worthwhile, “because it suits you”. I went along with it because I was like “eh, I’m still not sure what I want to do with myself yet, so why not?” and went along with it. It came as no surprise when I finished my first year of college that I hated it, knowing that EE 160 was not the end of the engineering nightmare. But I did come to learn of computer science, a major that focused more on coding AND had more realistic major requirements. I made the switch at the end of my second semester. 

<img class="ui tiny medium floated image" src="../images/essay1-code.JPG">

Switching was hard, since I had no close friends in the program and I was technically set back one year, but I don’t regret the switch at all. I have come to enjoy the tiny moments of happiness when my code compiles correctly, and the console prints out "``you did it! :D``". I aim to become more fluent in coding languages like Javascript and Python, collaborate on big ICS projects with my peers that I could show off, and hopefully one day create my own video game. I look forward to what my remaining years of college at UH have in store for me.
